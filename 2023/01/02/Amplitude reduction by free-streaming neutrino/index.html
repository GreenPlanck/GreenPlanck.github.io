WayneHu 1995a: Anisotropies in the cosmic microwave background: an analytic approach

Appendix A: Gravitational potentials and anisotrpoic stress

In this appendix, $a_0=a_{eq}=1$

The potential $\Phi$ is related with total density fluctuation $\Delta_T=\delta\rho_T/\rho_T$
$$
k^2\Phi=4\pi G\rho (a/a_0)^2\Delta_T
$$
The newtonian potential $\Psi$ is related to $\Phi$ through ansiotropic stress $\Pi$
$$
\Phi+\Psi=-\frac{8\pi Gp}{k^2}(a/a_0)^2\Pi
$$

$$
p\Pi=\frac{12}{5}(p_\gamma\Theta_2+p_\nu N_2)
$$
$\Theta_2$ vanishes due to the isotropizing effect of compton scattering in the tight coupling limit; $N_2$ can only be generated through free streaming from $N_0$.

The approach to obtain an analytic solution for $\Psi,\Phi$ is to include $\Pi$ as a small perturbation.

The perturbation equations for the total density and total velocity are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{\Delta}_T-3 w \frac{\dot{a}}{a} \Delta_T & =-(1+w) k V_T-2 \frac{\dot{a}}{a} w \Pi, \
\dot{V}_T+\frac{\dot{a}}{a} V_T & =\frac{4}{3} \frac{w}{(1+w)^2} k \Delta_T+k \Psi-\frac{2}{3} k \frac{w}{1+w} \Pi,
\end{aligned}
$$
where $w=p/\rho$ and $(\rho+p)V_T=\sum_i(\rho_i+p_i)V_i$, with $V_\gamma\equiv \Theta_1,V_\nu\equiv N_1$. I’m curious about how to get this two equations.

Here adiabatic conditions have been assumed and use the relations
$$
\Delta_\gamma=4\Theta_0+4\frac{\dot a}{a}\frac{V_T}{k}\
\Delta_\nu=4N_0+4\frac{\dot a}{a}\frac{V_T}{k}
$$
is used to convert from the Newtonian variables to the total matter rest frame variables.

Zeroth order larger scale solution

In the zeroth order $\Pi=0$. For superhorizon, we can ignore the $k\Delta_T$ pressure term in the velocity equation
$$
\begin{aligned}
\dot{\Delta}T^{(0)}-3 w \frac{\dot{a}}{a} \Delta_T^{(0)} & =-(1+w) k V_T^{(0)} \
\dot{V}T^{(0)}+\frac{\dot{a}}{a} V_T^{(0)} & =k \Psi^{(0)},
\end{aligned}
$$
The growing mode $\Delta_T^{(0)}(a)=A(k)U_G(a)$ is given by How to get this solution?
$$
U_G(a)=\left[a^3+\frac{2}{9} a^2-\frac{8}{9} a-\frac{16}{9}+\frac{16}{9} \sqrt{a+1}\right] \frac{1}{a(a+1)}
=\left{
\begin{split}
\frac{10}{9}a^2, &a\ll a{\rm eq} \text{radiation dominated}\
a,&a\gg a{\rm eq} \text{matter dominated}
\end{split},
\right.
$$
The decaying mode $U_D(a)=\frac{1}{a\sqrt{a+1}}$.

In this $\Pi=0$​ limit,
$$
\Phi\propto \rho (a/a_0)^2\Delta_T=
\left{
\begin{split}
\text{constant}, &a\ll a_{\rm eq} \text{radiation dominated}\
\text{constant},&a\gg a_{\rm eq} \text{matter dominated}
\end{split},
\right.
$$
the potential is constant in both the radiation and matter dominated limits. And $\Psi^{(0)}=-\Phi^{(0)}$, and it can be easily saw from the above equation that $\Psi^{(0)}(\rm today)=\frac{9}{10}\Psi^{(0)}(\rm initial)$

It is also useful to compare the acoustic amplitude to the Sachs-Wolfe effect (Sachs & Wolfe 1967) $|\Theta+\Psi|{r m s}\left(\eta_0, k\right)=\frac{1}{3} \Psi\left(\eta_0, k\right)$. With the relation (see HSa eq. A18)
$$
\Psi\left(\eta_0, k\right)=-\frac{9}{10}\left(1+\frac{4}{15} f_\nu\right)^{-1}\left(1+\frac{2}{5} f_\nu\right)^{-1} \Phi(0, k)
