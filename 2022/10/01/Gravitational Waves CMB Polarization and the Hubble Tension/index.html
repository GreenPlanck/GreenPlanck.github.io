Gravitational Waves CMB Polarization and the Hubble Tension

出发点

  1. 就像温度涨落功率谱上的峰来自于重子-光子的声学振荡，原初B-mode功率谱上也会有引力波传播留下的峰。由于传播速度不一样，两者尺度不一。
  2. 如何用B-mode 功率谱解决hubble tension? Hubble tension有两种思路，其一late-time的膨胀历史变了，其二，early-time new physics。如果Hubble tension源自晚期膨胀历史的变化，对T,B power spectrum的影响是一样的。故探测到的Bmode声学峰尺度应该和理论一致（因为T就一致）；如果是新物理，它对T B的声学视界是不一样的，也就实测值与理论值不一。

Idea

  1. 对于找$C_\ell^{BB}$$的peak location,取 $$\lambda_\alpha=\lambda_\beta=\ln \ell$

  2. 在某个噪声水平下，可探测的最小张标比(零假设r=0,导致$C_\ell^{BB}=0$)
    $$
    \sigma_r=\left[\sum_\ell\frac{(2\ell+1)f_{\rm sky}}{2}(\frac{\partial C_\ell/\partial r}{N_\ell})^2\right]^{-1/2}
    $$

  3. An interesting idea is to model the theory as $C_\ell=C_\ell^{\rm BB,theory}+\lambda C_\ell^{\rm BB,lens}$ to account for the imperfect substraction of lensing-induced B modes.

Appendix: $$1\sigma$$ error & Fisher Matrix[^ M]

$$\hat C\equiv\hat C_\ell,C\equiv C_\ell,Cov^{-1}\equiv Cov^{-1}_{\ell\ell’}$$

For some gaussian likelihood
$$
\ln \mathcal L=-\frac{1}{2}(\hat C-C)^TCov^{-1}(\hat C-C)
$$
$\hat C$ is the detected data and the covariance matrix both don’t rely on parameters. Thus the likelihood relies on parameters through mode $C$,
$$
\begin{gathered}
\frac{\partial \ln\mathcal L}{\partial \lambda_\alpha}=\frac{1}{2}
\left(\frac{\partial C}{\partial\lambda_\alpha}\right)^TCov^{-1}(\hat C-C)+\frac{1}{2}
(\hat C-C)
^TCov^{-1}\left(\frac{\partial C}{\partial\lambda_\alpha}\right)\\
-
<\frac{\partial^2 \ln\mathcal L}{\partial \lambda_\alpha\partial\lambda_\beta}> _{\lambda_\gamma=\bar\lambda_\gamma}=
\frac{1}{2}
\left(\frac{\partial C}{\partial \lambda_\alpha}\right)^T
Cov^{-1}
\left(\frac{\partial C}{\partial\lambda_\beta}\right)
+\frac{1}{2}
\left(\frac{\partial C}{\partial\lambda_\beta}\right)^T
Cov^{-1}
\left(\frac{\partial C}{\partial\lambda_\alpha}\right)
\end{gathered}
$$
where we have averaged at true parameters, making $\hat C-C$ vanish.

Then define Fisher matrix
$$
F_{\alpha\beta}=
-<\frac{\partial^2 \ln\mathcal L}
{\partial \lambda_\alpha\partial\lambda_\beta}> _{\lambda_\gamma=\bar\lambda_\gamma}
$$

The 1sigma error is gotten through $\sigma_\lambda=F_{\lambda\lambda}^{-1/2}$

The covariance matrix is
$$
\begin{gathered}
Cov_{\ell\ell’}=\frac{2}{2\ell+1}\left[C_\ell+N_\ell B_\ell^{-2}\right]^2\delta_{\ell\ell’}\\
\sigma_\ell^2=\frac{2}{(2\ell+1)f_{\rm sky}}\left[C_\ell+N_\ell B_\ell^{-2}\right]^2
\end{gathered}
$$
In this diagonal case, fisher matrix reduces to^2
$$
F_{\alpha\beta}=\sum_\ell\frac{1}{\sigma_\ell^2}\frac{\partial C_\ell(\lambda_0)}{\partial \lambda_\alpha}\frac{\partial C_\ell(\lambda_0)}{\partial \lambda_\beta}
$$

Polarization pursuers’ guide

Here, we are interested in the detectability of a polarization component; that is, what is the smallest polarization amplitude that we could confidently differentiate from zero?

This answer in detail depends on the full shape of the likelihood function: the ‘‘number of sigma’’ that the likelihood maximum lies away from zero is related to the fraction of integrated likelihood between zero and the maximum.

This gives an indication of how well the observation can be distinguished from zero power in the polarization. Toy problems and experience give us an approximate rule of thumb: the signal is detectable when it can be differentiated from the‘‘null hypothesis’’ of $C_{\ell}^{XX}=0$.

Stated another more Bayesian way, for a fixed noise variance, as we increase the observed signal the fraction of probability below the peak increases rapidly when the sample variance—i.e., the estimated power—approaches the noise.

Thus, on the one hand you need to observe enough sky to sufficiently decrease the sample variance, and a small enough noise that the sample variance dominates.
