2.2 Show that the geodesic equation gets the correct equations of motion for a particle traveling freely in two dimensions using polar coordinates. You can get the Christoffel symbols one of two ways (or both!) and then proceed to (b).
(a) Get the Christoffel symbol either directly from the term in brackets in Eq. (2.19) or from the 2D metric
$$
g_{ij}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0\
0 & r^2
\end{pmatrix}
$$
using Eq. (2.21). Show that the only nonzero Christoffel symbols are
$$
\Gamma^2_{\enspace 12}=\Gamma^2_{\enspace 21}=\frac{1}{r},\Gamma^1_{\enspace 22}=-r
$$
with 1, 2 corresponding to $r, \theta$ .

(b) Write down the two components of the geodesic equation using these Christoffel symbols. Show that these give the correct equations of motion for a particle traveling in a plane.

Solution:

(a)
$$
\Gamma^\mu_{\enspace\alpha\beta}=\frac{g^{\mu\nu}}{2}(g_{\alpha\nu,\beta}+g_{\beta\nu,\alpha}-g_{\alpha\beta,\nu})
$$
For $\mu=r$ :
$$
\Gamma^r_{\enspace\alpha\beta}=\frac{g^{rr}}{2}(g_{\alpha r,\beta}+g_{\beta r,\alpha}-g_{\alpha\beta,r})
$$

The $\alpha=r$ or $\beta=r$ components vanish because the $g_{rr}$ is constant and $g_{r\theta}=0$. The only nonzero term is $\alpha=\beta=\theta$,$\Gamma^r_{\enspace \theta\theta}=-g^{rr}g_{\theta\theta,r}/2=-r$

For $\mu=\theta$:
$$
\Gamma^{\theta}{\enspace \alpha\beta}=\frac{g^{\theta\theta}}{2}(g{\alpha\theta,\beta}+g_{\beta\theta,\alpha}-g_{\alpha\beta,\theta})
$$

Obviously terms, $\alpha=\beta=r$ and $\alpha=\beta=\theta$ , are zero. $\Gamma^\theta_{\enspace r\theta}=g^{\theta\theta}g_{\theta\theta,r}/2=1/r$.

Use the symmetry, we have $\Gamma^\theta_{\enspace r\theta}=\Gamma^\theta_{\enspace \theta r}=1/r$.

(b) The geodesic equation is
$$
\frac{d^2x^\mu}{d\lambda^2}+\Gamma^\mu_{\enspace \alpha\beta}\frac{dx^\alpha}{d\lambda}\frac{dx^\beta}{d\lambda}=0
$$
For $\mu=r$:
$$
\frac{d^2r}{d\lambda^2}-r(\frac{d\theta}{d\lambda})^2=0
$$

For $\mu=\theta$:
$$
\frac{d^2\theta}{d\lambda^2}+\frac{2}{r}\frac{dr}{d\lambda}\frac{d\theta}{d\lambda}=0
$$
We choose the affine parameter $\lambda $ as proper time $\tau $ for particles and because of $dt=\gamma d\tau$, the equations reduce to the exact Newton case in low speed limitation.

The four-dimensional energy momentum vector is $P^\alpha=(E,\vec P)$, which is defined through

E\frac{dE}{dt}=-\frac{\dot a}{a}(E^2-m^2)\Rightarrow \textcolor{red}{E=\sqrt{m^2+\frac{E_0^2}{a^2}}}
