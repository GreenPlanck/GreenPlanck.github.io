Phases of new physics in the CMB

发表于2022-12-30|更新于2022-12-30|CosmologyDR
|字数总计:2k|阅读时长:12分钟|阅读量:

A first detection of the acoustic oscillation phase shift expected from the CNB

It is believed that cosmic neutrino background contributes nearly as much as photons to the total energy density in the early universe. The neutrinos began to free stream at $T\sim {\rm MeV}$, unlike photons. Direct detection of CNB is thus difficult while an indirect evidence has existed for years according to the determination of the primordial abundance of Helium, which is affected by the contribution of neutrinos to the expansion rate ($N_{\rm eff}\rightarrow H\rightarrow Y_p$). More recently, CMB angular power spectrum’s damping tail serves as the evidence for CNB.

Phases of New Physics in the CMB

Section 2. Physical origin of the phase shift

The conformal Newtonian gauge
$$
ds^2=a^2(\tau)[(-1-2\Phi)d\tau^2+(1-2\Psi)\delta_{ij}dx^idx^j],
$$
and define the sum and difference of the two metric potentials
$$
\Phi_{\pm}=\Phi\pm\Psi.
$$
Define overdensity in the particle number with respect to both the proper volume, $\delta_a\equiv \delta\rho_a/(\bar\rho_a+\bar P_a)$, and the coordinate volume, $d_a$ .
$$
\delta_a\equiv \frac{\delta n_{a,\rm prop}}{n_{a,\rm prop}}\equiv \frac{\delta\rho_a}{\rho_a+p_a},d_a\equiv
\frac{\delta n_{a,\rm coo}}{n_{a,\rm coo}}
$$
This definition comes from the fact if $n$ is the density of any conserved number, then $d(nV)=0\rightarrow dn/n=-dV/V$. Given the energy conservation $d(\rho V)+pdV=0$, we have $dn/n=d\rho/(\rho+p).$

The variable $d_a$ can be interpreted in the Newtonian gauge as the perturbations of the conserved particle number densities with respect to the coordinate differential volume $d^3 r$ , rather than the proper volume $a^3(1-3\Psi)d^3r$.
$$
n_{\rm prop}\equiv\frac{dN}{dV_{\rm prop}}=\frac{dN}{a^3(1-3\Psi)d^3r}\
n_{\rm coo}\equiv \frac{dN}{dV_{\rm coo}}=\frac{dN}{d^3r}=a^3(1-3\Psi)N_{\rm prop}\
\frac{\delta n_{\rm coo}}{n_{\rm coo}}\overset{\triangle}{=}\frac{a^3(1-3\Psi)\delta n_{\rm prop}-a^33\Psi n_{\rm prop}}{a^3(1-3\Psi)n_{\rm prop}}=\delta_a-\frac{3\Psi}{1-3\Psi}=\delta_a-3\Psi
$$

Conservation of the stress-energy tensor for each decoupled species implies
$$
\dot \delta_a=\nabla^2 u_a+3\dot\Psi\
\dot u_a=c_a^2\delta_a-\chi_a u_a+\nabla^2\sigma_a+\Phi
$$
where $\chi_a\equiv \mathcal{H}(1-3c_a^2)$ is the Hubble drag rate.

If we take $c_a^2$ and $\chi_a$ independent of space, we can combine this two equations into one
$$
\ddot d_a+\chi_a\dot d_a-c_a^2\nabla^2d_a=\nabla^4\sigma_a+\nabla^2(\Phi+3c_a^2\Psi)
$$
To solve this equation requires additional equations for $\sigma_a, \Phi_\pm$.

To photons, it’s reduced to
$$
\ddot d_\gamma-c_\gamma^2\nabla^2 d_\gamma=\nabla^2\Phi_+,
$$
where $c_\gamma^2\simeq\frac{1}{3}$ thus no Hubble drag and no anistropic stress for tight-coupled photons. Since no scattering terms here, can this equation be used to describe photon-baryon plasma?

Boltzmann Equation

The evolution of phase space distributions obeys Boltzmann equation
$$
\dot f+\dot r_i\frac{\partial f}{\partial r_i}+\dot q\frac{\partial f}{\partial q}+\dot n_i\frac{\partial f}{\partial n_i}=(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \tau}){\rm C}
$$
Linearizing the Boltzmann equation relative to an unperturbed background phase space distribution $f=\bar f+\delta f$ in the Newtonian gauge,
$$
\dot{\delta f}+\frac{q_i}{\epsilon}\nabla_i(\delta f)+q\frac{\partial \bar f}{\partial q}(\dot \Psi-\frac{\epsilon}{q}n_i\nabla_i\Phi)=(\dot f){\rm c}-(\dot {\bar f})_{\rm c}
$$
where $\epsilon=\sqrt{q^2+a^2m^2}$. To eliminate $\dot \Psi$, we define a variable $df\equiv \delta f +q\frac{\partial \bar f}{\partial q}\Psi$,
$$
\dot{df_a}+\frac{q}{\epsilon}n_i\nabla_i(df_a)=q\frac{\partial \bar f}{\partial q}n_i\nabla_i(\frac{\epsilon}{q}\Phi+\frac{\epsilon}{q}\Psi)
$$
for collisionless particles.

For the massless case, $\epsilon \simeq q$, deining a function
$$
D_a\equiv \frac{3}{4}\frac{\int q^3dq df_a}{\int q^3dq \bar f_a}
$$
we now have
$$
\dot D_a+n_i\nabla_i D_a=-3n_i\nabla_i(\Psi+\Phi)
$$

Expand $D_a$ into mutipole moments
$$
D_a=\sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty}(-i)^\ell(2\ell+1)D_{a,\ell}P_\ell(\mu)
$$

$$
D_{a0}=d_a,D_{a1}=ku_a,D_{a2}=\frac{3}{2}k^2\sigma_a
$$

Einstein Equations

$$
\begin{aligned}
\ddot{\Psi}+\mathcal{H}(2 \dot{\Psi}+\dot{\Phi})+\left(2 \dot{\mathcal{H}}+\mathcal{H}^2\right) \Phi+\frac{1}{3} \nabla^2(\Phi-\Psi) & =4 \pi G a^2 \delta P \
\nabla^2 \Psi-3 \mathcal{H}(\dot{\Psi}+\mathcal{H} \Phi) & =4 \pi G a^2 \delta \rho,
\end{aligned}
$$

In terms of the fields $\Phi_\pm$, the first equation becomes
$$
(\ddot \Phi_+-\ddot \Phi_-)+\mathcal H(2(\dot \Phi_+-\dot \Phi_-)+(\dot \Phi_++\dot \Phi_-))+(2\mathcal {\dot H}+\mathcal H^2)(\Phi_++\Phi_-)+\frac{2}{3}\nabla^2\Phi_-=8\pi G a^2\delta P\
\Rightarrow\
\ddot \Phi_++3\mathcal H \dot \Phi_++(2\mathcal {\dot H}+\mathcal H^2)\Phi_+
=
8\pi Ga^2\delta P +S[\Phi_-]\
S[\Phi_-]=\ddot \Phi_- +\mathcal H \dot \Phi_- -(2\mathcal {\dot H}+\mathcal H^2+\frac{2}{3}\nabla^2)\Phi_-
$$
by using $\Phi=(\Phi_++\Phi_-)/2,\Psi=(\Phi_+-\Phi_-)/2$.

$\Phi_-$ is constrained by
$$
\Phi_-\overset{\triangle}{=}-12\pi Ga^2(\bar \rho+\bar P)\sigma
$$
In radiation domination
$$
\Phi_{+}^{\prime \prime}+\frac{4}{y} \Phi_{+}^{\prime}+\Phi_{+}\overset{\triangle}{=}
\frac{8 \pi G a^2}{\left(c_\gamma k\right)^2} \sum_a\left(c_a^2-c_\gamma^2\right) \delta \rho_a+\tilde{\mathcal{S}}\left[\Phi_{-}\right]
$$
where primes denote derivatives with respect to $y\equiv c_\gamma k \tau$ and $\tilde{\mathcal{S}}\left[\Phi_{-}\right] \equiv \Phi_{-}^{\prime \prime}+(2 / y) \Phi_{-}^{\prime}+3 \Phi_{-}$.

The Green’s function is
$$
\begin{equation}
G_{\Phi_{+}}\left(y, y^{\prime}\right)=\Theta\left(y-y^{\prime}\right) \frac{y^{\prime}}{y^3}\left[\left(y^{\prime}-y\right) \cos \left(y^{\prime}-y\right)-\left(1+y y^{\prime}\right) \sin \left(y^{\prime}-y\right)\right],
\end{equation}
$$
The time evolution of the source terms may require solving the Boltzmann equation.

Section 2.2 Conditions for a phase shift

$$
\ddot d_\gamma-c_\gamma^2\nabla^2 d_\gamma=\nabla^2\Phi_+\rightarrow d_\gamma’’+d_\gamma=-\frac{\Phi_+}{c_\gamma^2}
$$

The general solution for equations like $y’’(x)+y(x)=\delta(x-z)$ is
$$
y(x)=c_1\cos(x)+c_2\sin(x)+\Theta(x-z)\sin(x-z)
$$
With constant superhorizon initial condition $d_{\gamma,\rm in}\equiv d_\gamma(y_{\rm in}\ll 1)$,

the solution is
$$
\begin{equation}
d_\gamma(y)=d_{\gamma, \text { in }} \cos y-c_\gamma^{-2} \int_0^y \mathrm{~d} y^{\prime} \Phi_{+}\left(y^{\prime}\right) \sin \left(y-y^{\prime}\right)
\end{equation}
$$
By using $\sin \left(y-y^{\prime}\right)=\sin y \cos y^{\prime}-\cos y \sin y^{\prime}$, the solution can be written as
$$
\begin{equation}
d_\gamma(y)=\left[d_{\gamma, \text { in }}+c_\gamma^{-2} A(y)\right] \cos y-c_\gamma^{-2} B(y) \sin y
\end{equation}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& A(y) \equiv \int_0^y \mathrm{d} y^{\prime} \Phi_{+}\left(y^{\prime}\right) \sin y^{\prime}, \
& B(y) \equiv \int_0^y \mathrm{d} y^{\prime} \Phi_{+}\left(y^{\prime}\right) \cos y^{\prime} .
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus a nonzero $B$ will lead to a phase shift and a nonzero $A$ results in amplitude enhancement.

Two cases make $B\neq 0$: species traverl faster than $c_\gamma^2$; non-adiabatic fluctuations.

Section 2.4.2 Free streaming particles

Consider cases in the RD, and in the absence of additional pressure perturbations
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Phi_{+}^{\prime \prime}+\frac{4}{y} \Phi_{+}^{\prime}+\Phi_{+} & =\tilde{\mathcal{S}}\left[\Phi_{-}\right] \
& \equiv \Phi_{-}^{\prime \prime}+\frac{2}{y} \Phi_{-}^{\prime}+3 \Phi_{-} .
\end{aligned}
$$
Using the Green’s function, the solution
$$
\Phi_{+}(y)=3 \Phi_{+, \text {in }} \frac{\sin y-y \cos y}{y^3}+\int_{y_{\mathrm{in}}}^y \mathrm{d} y^{\prime} \tilde{\mathcal{S}}\left[\Phi_{-}\left(y^{\prime}\right)\right] G_{\Phi_{+}}\left(y, y^{\prime}\right)
$$
Write this solution as an expansion in powers of the fractional energy density contributed by the species of free-streaming particles,
$$
\epsilon_X\equiv \frac{\rho_X}{\rho_\gamma+\rho_X}=\frac{N_{\rm eff}}{a_\nu+N_{\rm eff}}
$$
To find the inhomogeneous part of the solution, we require $\Phi_-(y)$, which is determined by the anisotropic stress $\sigma_X$ induced by the free-streaming particles,
$$
\Phi_{-}(y)=-\frac{2 k^2 \epsilon_X}{y^2} \sigma_X(y)\overset{\triangle}=-\frac{4}{3} \frac{\epsilon_X}{y^2} D_{X, 2}(y)
$$
To determin $D_{X,2}$, we needs the linearized, collisionless Boltzmann equation
$$
\dot D_a+n_i\nabla_i D_a=-3n_i\nabla_i\Phi_+ \Rightarrow \dot D_X+ik\mu D_X=-3ik\mu\Phi_+
$$
It’s solution is
$$
D_X(\tau)=e^{-i k \mu\left(\tau-\tau_{\mathrm{in}}\right)} D_{X, \text { in }}-3 i k \mu \int_{\tau_{\mathrm{in}}}^\tau \mathrm{d} \tau^{\prime} e^{-i k \mu\left(\tau-\tau^{\prime}\right)} \Phi_{+}\left(\tau^{\prime}\right)
$$
What we want is the quadruple moment of this solution.
$$
D_{X, 2}(y)\overset{\triangle}=-3 \zeta j_2\left[c_\gamma^{-1} y\right]+\frac{3}{c_\gamma} \int_0^y \mathrm{d} y^{\prime} \Phi_{+}\left(y^{\prime}\right)\left{\frac{2}{5} j_1\left[c_\gamma^{-1}\left(y-y^{\prime}\right)\right]-\frac{3}{5} j_3\left[c_\gamma^{-1}\left(y-y^{\prime}\right)\right]\right}
$$

$\Phi_+\leftarrow \Phi_-\leftarrow D_{X,2}\leftarrow \Phi_+$

Now we can solve it perturbatively in $\epsilon_X$.

  • At zeroth order in $\epsilon_X$, $\Phi_-^{(0)}(y)=0$, hence $\Phi_+^{(0)}$ is given by its homogeneous part.

$$
\Phi_{+}^{(0)}(y)=4 \zeta \frac{\sin y-y \cos y}{y^3} .
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
A^{(0)}(y) & =2 \zeta-2 \zeta \frac{\sin ^2(y)}{y^2} \quad \stackrel{y \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} 2 \zeta, \
B^{(0)}(y) & =2 \zeta \frac{y-\cos y \sin y}{y^2} \stackrel{y \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} 0 .
\end{aligned}
$$
The result for the photon density perturbations then is
$$
d_\gamma^{(0)}(y) \approx 3 \zeta \cos y .
$$

  • At first order in $\epsilon _X$

$$
\Phi_{-}^{(1)}(y)=-\frac{4}{3} \frac{\epsilon_X}{y^2} D_{X, 2}^{(0)}(y)
$$

$$
D_{X, 2}^{(0)}(y)=-3 \zeta j_2\left[c_\gamma^{-1} y\right]+\frac{3}{c_\gamma} \int_0^y \mathrm{~d} y^{\prime} \Phi_{+}^{(0)}\left(y^{\prime}\right)\left{\frac{2}{5} j_1\left[c_\gamma^{-1}\left(y-y^{\prime}\right)\right]-\frac{3}{5} j_3\left[c_\gamma^{-1}\left(y-y^{\prime}\right)\right]\right}
$$

$$
\Phi_{+}^{(1)}(y)=-\frac{4}{15} \zeta \epsilon_X \frac{\sin y-y \cos y}{y^3}+\int_0^y \mathrm{~d} y^{\prime} \tilde{\mathcal{S}}\left[\Phi_{-}^{(1)}\left(y^{\prime}\right)\right] G_{\Phi_{+}}\left(y, y^{\prime}\right)
$$

The numerical results for $A,B$ are
$$
A^{(1)} \approx-0.268 \zeta \epsilon_X, \quad B^{(1)} \approx 0.600 \zeta \epsilon_X .
$$

Section 3

Let’s have a look what it says in the section 3.

$N_{\rm eff}$ and $N_{\rm fluid}$ are not distinguished by their effects on the damping tail. Future data sets will be much less sensitive to these degeneracies: Polarization is a direct measurement of the quadrupole at the surface of last-scattering while damping tail of temperature spectrum is the integrated effect. Thus future polarization measurement allows us to break the degeneracy between $Y_p$ and $N_{\rm eff}+N_{\rm fluid}$. Secondly, phase shift is not expected to be degenerate with other effects.
